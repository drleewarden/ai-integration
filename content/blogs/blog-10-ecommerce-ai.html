Australian e-commerce businesses are using AI to lift conversion rates, increase AOV, and improve retention — without adding to their paid acquisition budget. Here's what's working.

For most Australian e-commerce businesses, growth means spending more on advertising. CAC goes up. ROAS goes down. The only lever is to find more customers.

The businesses we're working with are finding a different lever: getting more revenue from the customers they already have. AI is central to that shift. Here are four areas where it's making a measurable difference.

1. Personalised recommendations that actually work

Off-the-shelf recommendation widgets (the "you might also like" section on most e-commerce sites) are nearly universally underpowered. They're typically based on simple co-occurrence data — what other customers bought alongside this product — without accounting for who this specific customer is, what they've browsed, or where they are in their purchase cycle.

A custom recommendations engine uses all of that context. It surfaces different products for a first-time visitor than for a customer who's bought twice. It accounts for browse behaviour within the current session, not just purchase history. It knows that a customer who just bought running shoes probably doesn't need another pair but might need running socks.

The outcome from our e-commerce case study: +35% conversion rate across the site. On a $5M annual revenue business, that's $1.75M additional revenue per year. The ad spend didn't change.

Where this makes sense: Businesses with 6+ months of transaction data, at least 500 SKUs, and returning customers. The more data, the better the model.

2. Customer lifetime value prediction and segment-based retention

Most e-commerce businesses know their average LTV. Few know which specific customers have high LTV potential that hasn't been realised yet.

LTV prediction models identify customers who look like your best customers at their current stage of the purchase cycle — and flag them for specific retention and upsell treatment before they become inactive.

The practical application: a customer who has bought twice in three months, browses frequently, and has a basket composition similar to your top 10% of customers by LTV is at high risk of becoming a very valuable customer — or of drifting if you don't engage them. The model identifies them. You decide what to do with the information.

What to do with the prediction:

  • Prioritise these customers in email personalisation
  • Trigger specific win-back sequences before they go inactive (not after)
  • Test loyalty or subscription offers on high-LTV segments

Typical outcomes: 15–25% improvement in repeat purchase rate for targeted segments. Reduction in churn in the 90–180 day post-first-purchase window.

3. Inventory and merchandising intelligence

Marking down the wrong products at the wrong time is one of the most expensive silent costs in e-commerce. It's usually driven by a combination of intuition, spreadsheet analysis that's already out of date, and seasonal convention.

AI inventory and merchandising models predict sell-through rates by SKU, flag overstock risk before it becomes a markdown requirement, and identify replenishment timing more accurately than reorder point formulas.

For fashion and seasonal businesses specifically, the model learns the velocity patterns for new SKUs faster than historical analysis — it can identify within the first two weeks of a new product's launch whether it's tracking toward a sell-through problem.

Typical outcomes: 20–30% reduction in markdown volume. Improvement in working capital position because overstock is caught earlier.

4. Post-purchase experience and replenishment triggers

The moment after a purchase is the highest-engagement moment in the customer lifecycle — and most businesses waste it with a generic order confirmation.

AI post-purchase systems personalise the experience at this moment: relevant complementary product recommendations based on what was bought (not generic best-sellers), replenishment reminders timed to the expected depletion of consumable products, and review requests triggered at the point of likely product satisfaction rather than a fixed number of days post-purchase.

Replenishment triggers alone are consistently high-ROI for businesses selling consumables. A customer who bought a 60-day supply of supplements is most receptive to a repurchase prompt at day 45, not day 30 or day 60. The model learns the right timing from historical repurchase data.

Typical outcomes: 15–20% increase in repurchase rate for consumable categories. Email CTR on replenishment triggers: 3–5× higher than generic promotional emails.

The common thread

All four of these applications share the same foundation: they use data the business already has more intelligently. No new traffic. No increased ad spend. The same customers, better served.

The limiting factor isn't usually the AI capability — it's the quality and organisation of the underlying data. Businesses with clean, well-structured customer and product data get results faster. Businesses with fragmented data need some consolidation work first.
