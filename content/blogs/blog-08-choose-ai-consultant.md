The Australian AI consulting market has grown faster than the quality controls have. There are excellent practitioners building real systems that deliver measurable outcomes. There are also a significant number of agencies selling AI strategy, AI expertise, and AI transformation with limited evidence of delivery capability behind the pitch.

This article gives you seven questions to ask any AI partner before you sign an engagement. We've written it from the perspective of what we'd want a potential client to ask us -- and what the right answers look like.

---

## 1. Can you show me a production system you've delivered -- not a case study, a system?

Case studies are written to sell. What you want to see is evidence of delivery: a working system, in a client's production environment, doing what it was built to do.

Ask for: a demonstration of a live system, or a technical description of a recent build specific enough that you could verify it. Specifics matter -- integrations used, architecture decisions made, problems encountered. A vague case study about "transformative outcomes" for an unnamed client in an unnamed industry is not evidence.

**What the right answer looks like:** A specific, demonstrable description of a recent system. Ideally, the ability to talk to the client who uses it.

---

## 2. Who specifically will work on our engagement?

Many consulting firms win business on the credibility of their principals and deliver it with junior consultants or offshore teams. This is more common in AI consulting than in most professional services categories because the demand for AI capability has outpaced supply.

Ask who will run the Discovery Sprint, who will architect the system, and who will be your primary contact throughout the engagement. Get the answer in writing in the proposal.

**What the right answer looks like:** Named individuals with verifiable backgrounds. Confirmation that those individuals, not a team member you haven't met, will be the primary practitioners on your project.

---

## 3. What is the specific, measurable success metric for this engagement?

If an AI partner can't give you a specific, measurable outcome target before the engagement begins, they can't be held accountable for delivering one.

"We'll improve your customer experience" is not a success metric. "We'll reduce tier-1 support ticket volume by 40% within 60 days of launch" is a success metric.

Ask this question early and watch how the partner responds. Vague answers at this stage are a reliable predictor of vague outcomes.

**What the right answer looks like:** A specific metric, a measurement method, and a timeline. Ideally, this is documented in the engagement agreement.

---

## 4. What happens if the system doesn't hit the success metric?

This is the accountability question. If the partner is confident in their work and in the metric they've proposed, they should have a clear answer.

Common non-answers: "We'll work with you to optimise it." "We're confident in the outcome." "We'll provide ongoing support." These are not answers to the question.

**What the right answer looks like:** "We stay involved until the metric is hit. That's the basis of our outcome rate."

---

## 5. Does the IP transfer to us, and does the system run on our infrastructure?

Proprietary platforms and hosted systems create long-term dependency on the vendor. If the system runs on their infrastructure, you're renting the outcome. If the code is theirs, you can't evolve the system without them.

Ask: who owns the code, the model, and the documentation? Does the system run on your infrastructure or theirs? Can your IT team maintain it? If you wanted to move to a different provider tomorrow, what would that cost?

**What the right answer looks like:** Full IP transfer on completion. System runs on your infrastructure. Your team can maintain it. No proprietary lock-in.

---

## 6. Is change management included -- or is it an add-on?

67–80% of AI implementations fail because the team doesn't adopt the system. Any partner who doesn't include change management as a standard delivery component is optimising for delivery, not for outcomes.

Ask specifically: what does your change management approach look like? Who is responsible for team training? How do you measure adoption?

**What the right answer looks like:** A specific, documented approach to change management built into the engagement scope, not sold separately.

---

## 7. Do you publish your pricing?

This isn't just about transparency -- it's a signal about how the firm operates.

Agencies that don't publish pricing are often pricing based on what they think you'll pay, not what the work costs. That's not necessarily bad-faith -- it's how much of the consulting industry works. But it creates incentives that don't align with your interests.

Agencies that publish pricing have to compete on the quality and outcomes of their work, not on their ability to read a client's budget.

**What the right answer looks like:** Published pricing ranges with clear explanations of what affects the cost. Or a willingness to give you numbers in the first conversation, not at the end of a sales cycle.
